# What Your Fights Are Actually About (Hint: It's Never the Dishes)

You've had this fight before. Maybe it was about the dishes. Maybe it was about being late. Maybe it was about how much time they spend on their phone or who forgot to pay the electric bill.

The specifics change. The feeling underneath doesn't. That's how you know the fight isn't about the dishes.

Every couple has 2 to 3 recurring conflicts that resurface in different disguises. The topic rotates but the emotional core stays the same. And until you identify that core, you'll keep having the same fight with different set decorations for the rest of your relationship.

## The Surface vs. The Engine

Think of a recurring fight as an iceberg. The visible portion, the thing you're arguing about, is the topic. Who left the garage door open. Why dinner isn't planned. Who forgot the school pickup schedule.

Below the waterline is the engine: the unmet emotional need that the topic is activating.

Here are the most common engines:

**"I don't feel prioritized."** This engine drives fights about phones, work hours, canceled plans, and forgotten commitments. The topic varies. The underlying message is the same: I don't feel like I matter enough to you. Something else always comes first.

**"I feel controlled."** This engine drives fights about spending, parenting styles, social plans, and household standards. The person feels like their autonomy is being restricted. Their choices are being overridden. They're not being treated as an equal partner but as someone who needs to be managed.

**"I don't feel respected."** This engine drives fights about tone, dismissive comments, eye-rolling, and being talked over. The person feels like their intelligence, opinions, or feelings are being devalued. The specific words matter less than the meta-message: you don't take me seriously.

**"I'm carrying too much."** This engine drives fights about chores, mental load, childcare distribution, and household management. The person isn't really angry about the trash can. They're exhausted from carrying a disproportionate share of the life management and they've hit their limit.

**"I don't feel wanted."** This engine drives fights about intimacy frequency, initiation patterns, physical affection, and attention. The person doesn't feel desired. Not just sexually. Desired as a whole person. Sought after. Chosen. The fight about "we never have sex anymore" is almost never about the sex. It's about feeling unwanted.

## How to Identify Your Engine

Next time you're in a fight that feels familiar, pause and ask yourself: what am I actually feeling right now?

Not what are you arguing about. What are you feeling. There's a difference.

If you're arguing about the dishes but feeling invisible, the fight is about being seen. If you're arguing about finances but feeling controlled, the fight is about autonomy. If you're arguing about their phone but feeling deprioritized, the fight is about mattering.

The emotional feeling is the engine. The topic is just the trigger.

You can also identify your engines by looking backward. Think about your last five arguments. Strip away the topics. What's the emotional theme? If three out of five fights left you feeling like your contributions weren't valued, your engine is recognition. If three out of five left you feeling like you didn't have a say, your engine is autonomy.

## Why You Keep Fighting About the Same Thing

Once you understand that the real conflict is an unmet emotional need, it becomes obvious why these fights recur. The need hasn't been met. So every new trigger that activates the same need produces the same fight.

You can resolve the dishes argument perfectly. You can create a chore chart, hire a cleaning service, buy a second dishwasher. But if the engine is "I don't feel prioritized," the dishes fight will just relocate to a different topic. Because the chore chart didn't address the need. It addressed the symptom.

This is why couples can go to therapy, get great advice about managing a specific conflict, implement the advice successfully, and still feel stuck. They solved the surface problem. The engine is still running.

## The Gottman Perpetual Problems

John Gottman's research found that 69% of conflicts in relationships are perpetual. They never get resolved. They're fundamental differences in personality, values, or needs that are managed, not eliminated.

This sounds depressing. It's actually liberating. Because it means most of your fights aren't solvable problems that you're failing to solve. They're ongoing dialogues about how two different people navigate life together.

The goal isn't to win the fight or even to resolve it. The goal is to understand the need underneath it and find ways to address that need, even if the surface topic keeps coming back.

"We'll always have different standards of cleanliness" is a perpetual problem. But "even though we're different, I want you to know I see how much effort you put into keeping the house up, and I don't take it for granted" addresses the engine. The mess might still bother her. But feeling seen and appreciated changes how she experiences the mess.

## How to Fight About the Real Thing

**Step 1: Name the engine out loud.** "I know we're arguing about the schedule, but I think the real issue for me is that I feel like my time doesn't get the same respect as yours." Naming the engine shifts the conversation from defensive surface-level debating to vulnerable truth-telling. It's harder. It's also the only path to progress.

**Step 2: Ask your partner about their engine.** "What are you actually feeling right now? Not what I did wrong. What are you feeling underneath this?" Give them time to answer. This question requires emotional processing that doesn't happen instantly.

**Step 3: Validate the engine before addressing the topic.** "I hear that you're feeling deprioritized. That makes sense given what's been happening this month. I don't want you to feel that way." Validate the feeling first. Then you can talk about logistics.

**Step 4: Address both layers.** The surface topic usually does need a practical solution. And the engine needs emotional attention. A good resolution handles both. "Let's figure out the schedule (surface). And I want to make sure you know that your time matters to me as much as mine (engine)."

## The Partner Mirror

Here's something couples rarely do but should: each person writes down what they think their partner's recurring emotional need is.

Not their own need. Their partner's.

Then swap. Compare what you wrote about each other with what they wrote about themselves. The gaps are illuminating. You might discover that you've been solving for the wrong engine entirely. You thought she wanted more help around the house. She actually wants to feel like you notice what she's already doing. Those require completely different responses.

## When Both Engines Fire at Once

The hardest fights happen when both partners have an engine running simultaneously.

She's feeling deprioritized. He's feeling controlled. She expresses her need ("you never put your phone down when I'm talking to you"). He hears it as a restriction on his autonomy ("now she's telling me what I can do with my phone"). Both engines are valid. But they're pointing in opposite directions. She needs more engagement. He needs less management.

When this happens, somebody has to go second. One person's engine gets addressed first. Then the other. Trying to address both simultaneously is how fights escalate into chaos.

The person who can say "let me hear your need first, and then I'll share mine" is displaying emotional maturity that transforms conflict from destructive to constructive. It's not easy. It's a skill. And it gets easier with practice.

## Tracking the Patterns

One of the most powerful uses of Closer is conflict pattern recognition. When both partners log how they're feeling daily, including moments of disconnection, the data reveals the engines. You can see that every time your stress spikes, your "feeling deprioritized" score drops. You can see that after certain types of arguments, one partner's satisfaction craters while the other's barely moves (indicating mismatched awareness).

Seeing the patterns in data is less threatening than having them pointed out in the heat of an argument. The data doesn't accuse. It just shows. And from there, you can have the real conversation about the real issue.

Stop fighting about the dishes. Start talking about what the dishes represent.

Align and grow together.
