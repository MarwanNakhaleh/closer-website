# Closer — Content Guidelines

Writing guidelines that apply to all Closer content: blog posts, TikTok scripts, marketing copy, and website text.

## Voice
Direct, warm, conversational. You're a friend who happens to know a lot about relationships. Talk TO the reader, not ABOUT concepts. Never sound like a therapist reading from a textbook.

## Sentence structure
- Short sentences. Punchy closers.
- Imperative mood: "Do this." "Try this." "Ask them."
- Heavy use of "you/your" — make it personal
- Lead with action, follow with why

## What to do
- Use plain language. Say "show up" not "demonstrate presence."
- Be specific. "Kiss them for six seconds" is better than "increase physical affection."
- Normalize the struggle. Most readers feel broken or behind. Reassure them.
- End with something that sticks. "Support is sexy." "Showing up is what matters." "That already puts you ahead."
- Mix individual tips (things one person can do) with couple tips (things to do together)
- Lean heavily into responsive desire — teach it without being clinical about it
- Short paragraphs. One sentence can be its own paragraph.

## What to avoid

### AI Tells
These patterns are widely recognized as AI writing and will undermine trust:

- No "It's not X, it's Y" contrast patterns (e.g., "It's not that you stopped loving each other"). Use affirmative statements instead.
- No hyphens in compound words (use "low pressure" not "low-pressure")
- No em dashes used as a crutch — if you catch yourself using more than one per paragraph, rewrite with periods
- No "Here's the thing:" or "Let's be honest:" openers
- No "In fact," or "The truth is," hedging
- No listicle headers that all follow the same grammatical pattern
- No "journey" or "navigate" or "landscape" or "leverage"

### Tone
- No clinical or academic tone. If referencing research, keep it conversational: "About 30% of women don't feel desire until after intimacy has already started. This is completely normal."
- No preachy or lecturing tone. Never "you should" or "you need to." Instead: "Try this" or "Why don't you..."
- No generic self-help filler ("communication is key", "be present", "practice mindfulness")
- No emojis in body copy
- Never make the reader feel ashamed, pressured, or talked down to

## Sample writing (founder's voice)

"Do one thing that makes your partner's life easier today. Don't let them ask you to do it, just do it for them. Support is sexy."

"Why don't you try simply asking your partner what they like? You can work together to enjoy the best pleasure possible. For the first ten minutes, make their pleasure your only priority. Follow their cues."

"Most couples go through dry spells. What matters is that you're here, paying attention, and willing to try. That already puts you ahead."

"If intimacy has dropped off, try saying: 'I miss being close to you. I just wanted you to know.' Naming the gap without blame opens the door."

## Audience
Committed couples in their 20s, 30s, and 40s. Primarily men who signed up to improve their sex lives with their partner. They're either in crisis mode (haven't been intimate in months) or maintenance mode (things are fine but could be better). They want practical help, not theory.

## Goal
After reading anything we write, the reader should feel inspired, curious, or reassured. Our ultimate goal is to help couples have better sex and reach a point where they no longer need us.
